Briefing Document: Advancing Climate-Resilient Infrastructure Through Standards, Finance, and Collaboration

### Executive Summary

The built environment faces escalating and increasingly complex risks from climate change, creating a shared sense of urgency across the engineering, commercial real estate, finance, and insurance sectors. Analysis of current efforts reveals a multi-pronged strategy is emerging to embed climate resilience into practice. This strategy combines the long-term, systematic rigor of formal standards development with faster, market-driven pathways and is underpinned by an expanding coalition of public and private stakeholders.

The formal process, led by organizations like the American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE) and ASTM International, is focused on evolving standards to be forward-looking. This involves a critical shift from relying on historical climate data to incorporating future climate projections for hazards such as extreme precipitation, flooding, and wind. However, this standards development and adoption cycle is a deliberate and lengthy process, often taking a decade or more from initial research to widespread jurisdictional enforcement.

In parallel, powerful market forces are accelerating the demand for climate-informed decision-making. The commercial property insurance crisis, characterized by rising costs and reduced availability of coverage, is a primary catalyst. It is forcing commercial real estate owners, lenders, and investors to quantify and mitigate physical climate risks to protect asset values and ensure deal viability. This has given rise to new frameworks like the ASTM E3429 _Guide for Property Resilience Assessments_, which seeks to standardize a chaotic landscape of climate risk reporting.

To bridge the gap created by the long standards timeline, practitioners and progressive jurisdictions are leveraging alternative pathways. Performance-based design, which uses advanced modeling to meet safety and resilience targets without strictly adhering to prescriptive rules, is gaining traction beyond its established use in seismic engineering. Concurrently, local building officials are using mechanisms like "director's rules" and dedicated offices for technical innovation to implement new science and methods far more rapidly than the national code cycle allows.

The central, cross-cutting theme is the imperative of collaboration. The formal partnership between ASCE and the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) serves as a foundational effort, which is now expanding to include other federal agencies (NIST, FEMA,USGS) and, critically, influential voices from the finance, banking, and insurance industries. The core challenge for this coalition is to move beyond qualitative arguments and build a compelling, quantitative business case for resilience. Success hinges on the co-development of integrated, accessible, and user-centric data tools that translate complex climate science into actionable information for designers, financiers, and policymakers.

## 1 The Evolving Landscape of Building Standards and Climate Risk

The foundation of safe construction in the United States--building codes and the technical standards they reference--is undergoing a fundamental transformation to address the non-stationarity of climate-related hazards. This evolution is marked by a deliberate shift from historical precedent to forward-looking risk assessment, a process that is both technically complex and governed by a lengthy development and adoption cycle.

### The Mandate for Forward-Looking Standards

Traditionally, design standards like ASCE 7, which dictates minimum loads for buildings, have been "backwards-facing," relying on the historical observational record to define environmental hazards. Recognizing the inadequacy of this approach in a changing climate, standards-developing bodies are actively working to integrate future conditions.

* **ASCE 7 Modernization:** The ASCE 7 committee is developing a new chapter on "Future Conditions" for its 2028 edition (ASCE 7-28). The focus is on hazards significantly influenced by climate change, including extreme temperature, rainfall, snow, wind, and flood.
* **FEMA's Flood Risk Evolution:** The Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) is moving beyond the traditional binary, "100-year hazard basis" for flood risk, which has proven inadequate. The new approach embraces a "multiple return period" model to provide more consistent levels of protection, aligning flood risk management with how other hazards are treated.
* **Future Flood Risk Data (FFRD):** To support this new approach, FEMA is developing the FFRD initiative. This aims to provide richer, site-specific data that goes beyond simple inundation maps to include flood velocities, probabilistic return periods well beyond the 500-year event, and data on combined hazards from multiple sources (e.g., coastal, pluvial, levee-related).

### The "10-Year Challenge": The Standards Development and Adoption Cycle

The process of translating scientific research into enforceable building code is methodical and protracted, posing a significant challenge in a rapidly changing environment.

1. **Research and Data Development (1-4+ years):** Federal partners like NOAA, NIST, FEMA, and USGS conduct foundational research and develop the hazard data that underpins standards.
2. **Standards Development (5-6 years):** An ANSI-accredited consensus process is used by organizations like ASCE to develop or update a standard (e.g., ASCE 7).
3. **Code Adoption (3 years):** The updated standard is proposed for inclusion in a model building code, such as the International Building Code (IBC).
4. **Jurisdictional Adoption (3-6+ years):** State and local governments must individually adopt the new version of the model code for it to become law.

This entire pipeline can easily span **10 to 12 years**. For instance, work being done now for ASCE 7-28 is not expected to be widely enforced by jurisdictions until approximately 2032. To incorporate new NOAA climate data into the subsequent cycle (ASCE 7-34), that data must be finalized and available between 2028 and 2030 for potential enforcement around 2038. This reality underscores the critical importance of aligning federal research and data production timelines with the long-term needs of the standards community.

### What Standards Provide

It is crucial to understand the specific role of these documents.

* ASCE standards define the **minimum criteria for performance** to protect public health, safety, and welfare.
* The ASTM E3429 standard is a **guide**, defined as a framework to raise awareness and outline a process, distinct from a prescriptive specification or test method.
* These standards are **not** a certification of resilience, an audit of building code compliance, a greenhouse gas assessment, or a financial disclosure document.

## 2 Market-Driven Impetus from Finance and Insurance

While the standards development process advances methodically, the financial and insurance markets are creating immediate and powerful incentives for climate resilience. Increasing concerns over asset value, insurability, and regulatory pressure are forcing the commercial real estate (CRE) sector to proactively assess and manage physical climate risk.

### The Rise of the Property Resilience Assessment (ASTM E3429)

The CRE finance community, which has long relied on standardized due diligence tools like the Phase One Environmental Site Assessment (ESA) and the Property Condition Assessment (PCA), found itself facing a chaotic market of climate risk reports. Different providers offered varied, often opaque assessments, making apples-to-apples comparisons impossible. To address this, the ASTM E3429 _Guide for Property Resilience Assessments_ (PRA) was developed to create a common language and bring transparency and consistency to the market.

The PRA outlines a three-stage, multi-hazard process:

* **Stage 1: Screening:** A desktop assessment using data from commercial providers to screen a property for a range of hazards. A key component of this stage is user engagement to define the scope and risk tolerance, and it requires providers to disclose their methodologies and sources of uncertainty.
* **Stage 2: Property Visit:** A "boots on the ground" site visit by a qualified engineer or architect to assess building-specific attributes and vulnerabilities that cannot be seen in desktop models. This stage also evaluates operational preparedness. The guide expresses a strong preference for quantitative over qualitative findings.
* **Stage 3: Resilience Measures:** If necessary, this stage identifies potential resilience measures to mitigate the identified risks.

### The Insurance Crisis as a Catalyst

The most significant driver of this market shift is the crisis in the commercial property insurance sector.

* **Deal Viability:** Insurability has become a critical factor in CRE transactions. One cited example involved a property valued at over $1 billion where the rating agency required $800 million in insurance coverage. The market was only willing to provide $400 million, causing the deal to stall.
* **Financial Impact:** Insurance is priced annually and is a key component of a property's Net Operating Income (NOI). Skyrocketing insurance premiums directly reduce NOI, which in turn lowers a property's appraised value. This creates a direct financial feedback loop where higher risk leads to lower asset value.
* **Stakeholder Pressure:** Lenders, investors, developers, and rating agencies are all now intensely focused on climate risk and insurability before making loans or investments.

## 3 Putting Knowledge into Practice: Overcoming the Timeline

Given the 10+ year standards adoption cycle, practitioners and jurisdictions cannot wait for new codes to address urgent climate risks. In response, they are utilizing alternative regulatory and design frameworks to implement new science and engineering knowledge more rapidly.

### Performance-Based Design (PBD) as an Alternative Pathway

Performance-Based Design offers a powerful alternative to traditional prescriptive codes. Instead of following a set of explicit rules, a PBD approach uses advanced modeling and analysis to demonstrate that a proposed design meets the underlying performance objectives of the code (e.g., a specific, low probability of collapse in a major earthquake).

* **Established Precedent:** PBD is the standard of care for tall buildings in high-seismic zones on the West Coast. It allows engineers to design structures that are safer and more efficient--often at a lower cost and with less material--than prescriptive methods would allow.
* **Emerging Applications:** The concepts of PBD are now being applied to other hazards, most notably in the form of Performance-Based Wind Design.
* **Overcoming Fear:** A primary challenge to adopting PBD is fear--of the unknown, of liability, and of being the first. This is mitigated through industry consensus building, robust peer review processes, and early, collaborative engagement between the design team and the building official.
Local jurisdictions possess the authority to adopt new requirements and methods outside the national code update cycle. This local flexibility is a key mechanism for accelerating the implementation of new resilience knowledge.

* **Direct Regulatory Action:** The City of Seattle provides a clear example. Upon learning from new research that seismic ground motions from the Seattle Basin were worse than codes assumed, the city convened a panel of experts and quickly issued a **"Director's Rule"** requiring designs to account for this new data, years ahead of any formal code update.
* **Dedicated Innovation Offices:** New York City established an **Office of Technical Certification and Research** specifically to evaluate and approve alternative materials, designs, and construction methods. The office publishes **"Buildings Bulletins"** that provide a predictable and transparent pathway for using innovative solutions, provided they are demonstrated to be "equally safe" to code-prescribed methods.

## 4 The Imperative of Collaboration and Co-Development

Addressing the systemic challenge of climate change requires a level of collaboration that transcends traditional institutional silos. The engineering and scientific communities are formalizing partnerships and expanding their engagement to include the financial sector, creating a broader coalition to advocate for and implement resilient infrastructure.

### The ASCE-NOAA Memorandum of Understanding (MOU)

Signed in 2023, the MOU between ASCE and NOAA creates a formal framework to improve the flow of climate information and services to the civil engineering profession. With the University of Maryland serving as a "boundary organization," the partnership aims to ensure that NOAA's climate science is effectively translated and integrated into ASCE's standards, manuals of practice, and educational content. This collaboration is designed to establish direct links between NOAA's scientific offices and ASCE's technical institutes.

### Expanding the Coalition

The initial focus on connecting climate scientists and engineers has broadened significantly.

* **Inter-Agency Cooperation:** Other key federal agencies, including NIST, FEMA, USGS, and NASA, are now active participants in the dialogue, bringing expertise in everything from building science and disaster response to economic modeling.
* **Integrating the Financial Sector:** The most critical expansion has been the inclusion of the insurance, reinsurance, finance, and banking industries. Their participation is essential for building the business case for resilience and creating the financial incentives that drive changes in practice.
Through this expanded dialogue, a clear consensus has emerged on the most pressing technical needs.

* **Beyond "Another Tool":** There is a strong demand from users to move away from a proliferation of standalone data tools. The priority is the **co-development of integrated data platforms** that seamlessly combine historical data, forward-looking models, physical risk data, and economic information.
* **A "User-to-User" Process:** The most effective path forward is a process that starts with the end-user. It involves clearly defining the specific variables, units, and formats that designers and decision-makers need, and then working with data providers like NOAA to customize and deliver that information in a usable format.
* **Education and Workforce Development:** A long-term goal is to adapt engineering and finance education to produce a future generation of professionals who are comfortable working at the intersection of these fields, equipped to navigate the complex, interdisciplinary challenges of building a resilient society.
